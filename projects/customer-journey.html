The Problem

Where are customers dropping off — and which channels actually drive conversions?

Retention programs and paid media budgets were being allocated on rules of thumb rather than evidence. This project provides a quantitative view of where customers drop out of the conversion funnel and which marketing channels actually earn their conversions.

Working with an Atlanta-based sponsor, the team analyzed session-level event data to understand how customers interact with the platform, identify drop-off points in the conversion funnel, segment customers by behavior and lifetime value, and assess retention trends across cohorts.

The analysis informs three decisions: where to invest in funnel optimization, which customer segments to prioritize for retention, and how to reallocate channel budgets based on causal contribution rather than last-touch credit.

Methodology

A four-phase analytical approach

01

ETL & Sessionization

Sessions were classified into five journey stages — Landing, Browse, Product View, Add to Cart, Purchase — using a custom classify_journey function. Transition counts between stages were computed to build transition matrices for the journey network graph.

02

Funnel Analysis

Stage-to-stage conversion rates and drop-off percentages were calculated across all 681,864 sessions. Two parallel paths to Product View were identified: through Browse (63.3% of sessions) and directly from Landing (36.7%).

03

Customer Segmentation

Customers segmented into three behavioral tiers based on lifetime order count: One-time (1 order), Returning (2–3 orders), and Loyal (4+ orders). LTV, order count, and active months computed per segment.

04

Markov Chain Attribution

A Markov chain with removal-effect credit allocation was built across eleven states: Start, eight marketing channels, Conversion, and Null. For each channel, its row in the transition matrix was zeroed out and the resulting drop in conversion probability was measured — this is the removal effect. Normalizing across channels yields percentage attribution.

Recommendations

Four actions, ordered by expected impact

01

Fix checkout drop-off

A/B test shipping-cost display, form length, and payment options. Closing a tenth of the 57.9% gap yields roughly 25,000 more converted sessions on observed volume.

02

Grow the Returning segment

Already the largest revenue contributor at 43.7%. Lifecycle marketing to convert One-time buyers to Returning is the most direct path to growth — a 2.2x LTV lift per conversion.

03

Intervene in the first 30 days

Recent cohorts drop to 18% month-1 retention against a 30–33% historical baseline. A targeted email or offer inside the first month addresses the highest-impact churn window.

04

Operationalize Markov attribution as a merchant service

Package the attribution pipeline as a repeatable offering. Inputs are ordered channel sequences and a conversion label; outputs are removal-effect channel ranks and attributed revenue — letting spend decisions rest on modeled contribution rather than last-touch heuristics.
